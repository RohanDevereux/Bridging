\begin{abstract}
This thesis studies protein--protein binding affinity prediction from structure and asks one question: does a short explicit-solvent molecular-dynamics simulation improve a residue-graph affinity model over a strong static baseline? The pipeline starts from a curated wild-type subset of PPB-Affinity, prepares each complex for molecular dynamics, runs a short OpenMM simulation, constructs residue graphs with DeepRank2, adds trajectory-derived node and edge summaries, and evaluates either a masked graph variational autoencoder or a direct supervised graph regressor.

The starting processed table contains 2818 complexes. The final model matrix was run on a smaller compute-bounded 1437-complex analysis subset for which paired full and interface-restricted residue graphs were available. The matrix contains 52 configurations: 48 GraphVAE configurations and 4 supervised baselines. Each configuration is evaluated over 10 outer held-out fits from 5 folds and 2 repeats.

The strongest model is the direct supervised baseline on the full static graph, with mean test RMSE 2.3379, Pearson correlation 0.5064, and $R^2$ 0.2146. Within the GraphVAE family, the best configuration is the full static semi-supervised model with latent dimension 32, with mean test RMSE 2.4292. The full graph outperforms the interface-restricted graph in all 24 matched GraphVAE comparisons. Under the shared-static reconstruction target, the static representation outperforms the static-plus-dynamic representation in 11 of 12 matched GraphVAE comparisons.

For this pipeline, the molecular-dynamics-derived summaries do not improve held-out affinity prediction over a static residue graph. The best-performing representation among the tested models is the full static residue graph.
\end{abstract}
