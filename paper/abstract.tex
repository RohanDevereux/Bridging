\begin{abstract}
Protein--protein binding affinity measures how strongly two proteins associate and is important for protein design, therapeutic targeting, and drug discovery. Most fast predictors estimate affinity from a single bound structure, even though binding is an ensemble property and proteins can adopt more than one relevant arrangement. This study tests whether short explicit-solvent molecular dynamics (MD) can add useful information to a residue-graph affinity predictor.

Starting from a curated wild-type subset of PPB-Affinity, each retained complex was prepared for MD, simulated in OpenMM, converted to a residue graph, and enriched with trajectory-derived summary features. The final controlled comparison used 1437 complexes and 52 models, combining full or interface-restricted graphs, static or static-plus-dynamic features, and two model families: a masked GraphVAE with affinity predicted from exported latent means by ridge regression, and a direct supervised graph regressor. All models were evaluated over 10 outer held-out fits.

The strongest model was the direct supervised baseline on the full static graph, with mean test RMSE $2.3379 \pm 0.1067$, Pearson correlation $0.5064 \pm 0.0567$, and $R^2 = 0.2146 \pm 0.0891$. The full graph outperformed the interface-restricted graph in all 24 matched GraphVAE comparisons, and static features outperformed static-plus-dynamic features in 11 of 12 matched GraphVAE comparisons under the static-only reconstruction target.

On this benchmark, the static whole-complex representation was strongest. The results suggest that 1.2 ns trajectories captured little new affinity-relevant motion, while coarse trajectory summaries made any weak added signal difficult to use reliably.
\end{abstract}
