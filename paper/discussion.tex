\chapter{Discussion and Conclusions}
\label{ch:discussion}

\section{Evaluation context}

Published protein--protein predictors have reported higher correlations on their own evaluation sets than the best model reached here. Moal et al.\ reported $r=0.73$ on a diverse 137-complex benchmark, PPB-Affinity reported $r=0.701$ on its full test set, and PPI-Graphomer reported $r=0.641$ and $r=0.625$ on two external sets \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction,Liu2024PPBAffinity,Xie2025PPIGraphomer}. The best model here reached $r=0.506$ on the matched 1437-complex subset. The main contribution is methodological. It lies in a controlled comparison of representation scope, dynamic features, and model family within one benchmark, one graph-construction procedure, and one repeated held-out evaluation design.

That evaluation design is part of the contribution. Earlier work showed that score changes can come from the split as well as from the model \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction,RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity,Graber2025DataBias}. The repeated 2$\times$5 outer evaluation used here reduces the chance that one favourable split will dominate the conclusion. Across the held-out fits, the same broad ordering and the same factor effects recur even though the top two full-graph models remain fairly close on a few splits.

\section{Representation scope}

The full residue graph outperformed the interface-restricted representation. The full view won all 24 matched GraphVAE comparisons and both supervised baseline comparisons. Whole-complex context appears to help the model recognise which partners are present, how the two chains are arranged in space, and what broader structural setting surrounds the binding site. A fixed 128-residue interface window keeps only part of that information. On this benchmark, the extra context was more useful than a tighter focus on the contact region. The pattern is closer to whole-complex or hybrid approaches such as Moal et al., PPI-Affinity, and PPI-Graphomer than to strongly interface-focused predictors such as PRODIGY and the PPB-Affinity benchmark model \citep{Vangone2015Contacts,Moal2011AffinityPrediction,Liu2024PPBAffinity,RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity,Xie2025PPIGraphomer}. Under the same encoder family and repeated held-out evaluation, the whole-complex view was the stronger choice.

\section{Dynamics and timescale}

The static feature set remained stronger than the dynamic features. The comparison between $S$ and $SD$ was intentionally controlled. Both modes started from the same post-prep graph, so the only extra information in $SD$ came from the trajectory summaries. Within that shared setup, the family-level results show the same pattern in both the full and interface semi-supervised families. Adding dynamic features improved train fit but weakened validation and test performance. In these families, the extra dynamic information increased overfitting without improving held-out prediction.

Trajectory length is the most likely reason. Each complex contributed 1.2 ns of saved MD, which is enough to capture local fluctuation and contact persistence but is short relative to many rearrangements that shape protein binding \citep{HenzlerWildman2007Dynamic,Kastritis2011Benchmark,Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview}. Slow collective motions often extend to microseconds and slower \citep{HenzlerWildman2007Dynamic}. These trajectories were therefore unlikely to sample much of the slower motion that distinguishes one binding ensemble from another. Limited added signal is the most plausible explanation for the weak dynamic-feature results.

Feature design is another part of the story. Recent protein--ligand work suggests that MD becomes more useful when the model keeps richer temporal or ensemble information instead of reducing each trajectory to a few averages \citep{Min2024Dynaformer,Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}. Here, each short trajectory was compressed to residue- and edge-level summary features on a fixed graph. That decision made the comparison tractable at scale, but it also meant that temporal order was not represented in the graph at all and that each dynamic channel was reduced to a small set of moments or frequencies. The dynamic idea remains plausible. These summaries did not retain enough stable new signal to improve affinity prediction.

\section{Supervision}

Semi-supervision made the GraphVAE latents much more useful for affinity prediction than masked reconstruction alone. The unsupervised families had low correlations on train, validation, and test, together with small train--test gaps. That pattern is better explained by weak affinity signal in the learned latent space than by simple overfitting. Once the affinity objective was added, the same encoder family produced much stronger downstream correlations in nearly every matched comparison.

This result suggests that the masked reconstruction task was learning graph structure, but not organising the latent space in a way that was strongly aligned with binding affinity. The auxiliary affinity loss changed that alignment. In this study, the latent representation did not become affinity-relevant automatically. It became much more affinity-relevant once training was biased toward the target.

\section{Model family}

The masked GraphVAE remained competitive, and the best semi-supervised variants were close to the strongest baseline. The full static baseline still produced the strongest held-out predictor. A likely explanation is that direct supervised training can spend all of its capacity on affinity, whereas the VAE also has to spend capacity reconstructing masked graph features. In this comparison, that trade-off left the direct supervised path with the stronger affinity predictor. Variational models can still learn useful protein latent spaces in other settings \citep{Kingma2014VAE,Kipf2016VGAE,Tian2021ProteinVAE}.

\section{Interaction classes}

Interaction classes also differed meaningfully. On pooled outer-test predictions, the best overall model, the full static baseline, gave Pearson $r=0.292$ on antibody--antigen, $r=0.430$ on the broad other class, and $r=0.466$ on TCR-pMHC. The full static baseline remained the strongest of the main models in all three interaction classes. This matches PPB-Affinity's subgroup behaviour and later antibody-specific work arguing that antibody--antigen complexes benefit from specialised treatment \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity,Yang2023AntibodyAffinity}. A pooled benchmark average can hide those differences. A model can look competitive overall and still struggle on an interaction class of practical interest. The antibody--antigen correlations here are lower than in the broad other class. The TCR-pMHC subgroup is also small enough that its uncertainty remains large.

\section{Limitations}

Time and compute shaped the scale of the study. An ideal version of this comparison would have carried the full 2818-complex curated table through longer and more carefully tailored simulations. That was not feasible here because explicit-solvent MD at this scale was too slow for the available budget. The final controlled comparison therefore used a 1437-complex matched subset and 1.2 ns trajectories per complex. PPB-Affinity itself reported stronger performance as benchmark scale increased \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}, so the reduced analysed set likely limited absolute predictive power even before the MD question is considered.

The dynamic features also carry noise from how the simulations had to be prepared and summarised. To keep enough complexes in the study, preparation was standardised across complexes, missing residues were not rebuilt, protonation was standardised, and each short trajectory was compressed to residue- and edge-level summary statistics on a fixed graph. Those choices made the comparison feasible, but they also combine thermal fluctuation, finite sampling, and summary-statistic noise. Using the same post-prep graph for both $S$ and $SD$ reduced noise in the comparison itself, because both modes started from the same structure source. That same choice may also leave the absolute static-only numbers below what graphs built directly from raw experimental coordinates could achieve.

The retained 1437-complex set is enriched for antibody--antigen complexes and slightly stronger binders, so the reported numbers apply to this matched subset and not to the full PPB-Affinity benchmark. The repeated resampling was done by complex identifier only, leaving homology-aware and structure-aware separation unconstrained, so related protein families could still appear across train and test. Earlier protein--protein studies removed homologous complexes for this reason, and similar leakage problems have been documented in protein--ligand affinity prediction \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction,Graber2025DataBias}. Transfer to unbound or predicted inputs remains open because only bound complex structures were used here \citep{Guo2022PPBAReview}.

\section{Future work}

A stronger test of dynamics would use longer or better-targeted trajectories, especially if the goal is to capture slower binding-related motion as well as local fluctuation. More careful structure preparation would also help, but that would require substantially more manual preparation work across a benchmark of this size. Recent protein--ligand work suggests that MD becomes more useful when the model keeps richer temporal or ensemble information instead of reducing the trajectory to a few averages \citep{Min2024Dynaformer,Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}.

Richer structure and evaluation settings would also be useful. One shared post-prep graph was used for both $S$ and $SD$ so that the incremental effect of the trajectory summaries stayed isolated. Comparing that controlled design with graphs built directly from raw experimental coordinates would show how much the shared post-prep source affects absolute static performance. Stricter family-aware splits and the same representation choices on unbound or predicted structures would also be useful, since practical affinity prediction is often harder in those settings.

\section{Conclusion}

On this benchmark, a whole-complex static residue graph was the strongest starting point for protein--protein affinity prediction. In the 52-model comparison, graph scope had a larger effect on performance than the dynamic features added here. The full static baseline beat every other model. The dynamic variants more often improved training fit than held-out prediction. For this benchmark, strong whole-complex static models and careful repeated evaluation are the best starting point. Dynamics become more attractive when longer simulations or models that retain temporal structure can justify the extra complexity.
