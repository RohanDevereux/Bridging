\chapter{Discussion and Conclusions}
\label{ch:discussion}

\section{Main findings}

First, the full residue graph outperformed the interface-restricted representation. The full view won all 24 matched GraphVAE comparisons and both supervised baseline comparisons. Whole-complex context appears to help the model recognise which partners are present, how the two chains are arranged in space, and what broader structural setting surrounds the binding site. A fixed 128-residue interface window keeps only part of that information. On this benchmark, the extra context was more useful than a tighter focus on the contact region. This contrasts with interface-focused predictors such as PRODIGY and the PPB-Affinity benchmark model and is closer in spirit to whole-complex or hybrid approaches such as Moal et al., PPI-Affinity, and PPI-Graphomer \citep{Vangone2015Contacts,Moal2011AffinityPrediction,Liu2024PPBAffinity,RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity,Xie2025PPIGraphomer}. Under the same encoder family and repeated held-out evaluation, the whole-complex view was the stronger choice.

Second, the static feature set remained stronger than the MD-derived summaries. The comparison between $S$ and $SD$ was intentionally controlled: both modes started from the same post-prep graph, so the only extra information in $SD$ came from the trajectory summaries. Within that shared setup, the family-level results show the same pattern in both the full and interface semi-supervised families. Adding dynamic summaries improved train fit but weakened validation and test performance. The added MD summaries therefore gave the models more variation to fit without improving held-out prediction.

Third, the masked GraphVAE remained competitive, and semi-supervised training improved most of the VAE families over the unsupervised variants. The exception was the interface dynamic family, where the effect was mixed. The full static baseline still produced the strongest held-out predictor. A likely explanation is that direct supervised training can spend all of its capacity on affinity, whereas the VAE also spends capacity reconstructing masked graph features. That trade-off still produced useful latent spaces, but direct supervised training was more effective for affinity prediction in this comparison \citep{Kingma2014VAE,Kipf2016VGAE,Tian2021ProteinVAE}.

Fourth, the main conclusions were stable under repeated held-out evaluation. The same design choices stayed favourable across 10 outer fits, and the family-level summaries support the same ranking as the leading models. The full static baseline beat the best GraphVAE in 8 of 10 outer fits, so the top of the ranking is consistent even though the two best full-graph models remain fairly close.

Different interaction classes also remained visibly different in the final comparison. On pooled outer-test predictions, the best overall model, the full static baseline, gave Pearson $r=0.292$ on antibody--antigen, $r=0.430$ on the broad other class, and $r=0.466$ on TCR-pMHC. The full static baseline remained the strongest of the main models in all three interaction classes. This matches PPB-Affinity's subgroup behaviour and later antibody-specific work arguing that antibody--antigen complexes benefit from specialised treatment \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity,Yang2023AntibodyAffinity}. The pooled benchmark can hide real differences between interaction classes: a model can look competitive overall and still struggle on an interaction class that matters in practice. The antibody--antigen correlations here are clearly weaker than the broad other class, while the TCR-pMHC subgroup is small enough that its uncertainty remains large.

\section{Limitations}

Trajectory length was the main limitation. Each complex contributed 1.2 ns of saved MD, which is enough to capture local fluctuation and contact persistence but is short relative to many rearrangements that shape protein binding \citep{HenzlerWildman2007Dynamic,Kastritis2011Benchmark,Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview}. Slow collective motions often extend to microseconds and slower \citep{HenzlerWildman2007Dynamic}, so these trajectories were unlikely to sample much of the slower motion that distinguishes one binding ensemble from another. That makes limited added signal the most likely explanation for the weak dynamic-feature results.

The dynamic features also carry noise from how the MD was run and summarised. With more time and compute, preparation could be refined case by case and the trajectories could be kept in a richer form. Doing so would have reduced the number of complexes that could be carried through the comparison. To keep the study large enough for machine learning, preparation was standardised across complexes, missing residues were not rebuilt, protonation was standardised, and each short trajectory was compressed to residue- and edge-level summary statistics on a fixed graph. Those choices made a 1437-complex comparison feasible, but they also combine thermal fluctuation, finite sampling, and summary-statistic noise. Using the same post-prep graph for both $S$ and $SD$ reduced noise in the comparison itself, because both modes started from the same structure source. That same choice may also leave the absolute static-only numbers below what graphs built directly from raw experimental coordinates could achieve.

The final controlled comparison also used a 1437-complex matched subset drawn from the 2818-complex curated table. That retained set is enriched for antibody--antigen complexes and slightly stronger binders, so the reported numbers should be read as results on this matched subset rather than as a direct replacement for the full PPB-Affinity benchmark. The repeated resampling was done by complex identifier only, leaving homology-aware and structure-aware separation unconstrained, so related protein families could still appear across train and test. Earlier protein--protein studies removed homologous complexes for this reason, and similar leakage problems have been documented in protein--ligand affinity prediction \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction,Graber2025DataBias}. Transfer to unbound or predicted inputs remains open, since the present study uses bound complex structures in which both proteins are already observed together \citep{Guo2022PPBAReview}.

\section{Future work}

Future work should give MD a better chance to help. That means longer or better-targeted trajectories, especially if the goal is to capture slower binding-related motion as well as local fluctuation. It also means cleaner structure preparation where the compute budget allows it. Recent protein--ligand work suggests that MD becomes more useful when the model keeps richer temporal or ensemble information instead of reducing the trajectory to a few averages \citep{Min2024Dynaformer,Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}.

Future work should also test richer structure and evaluation settings. The current study used one shared post-prep graph for both $S$ and $SD$ to isolate the incremental effect of the trajectory summaries. Comparing that controlled design with graphs built directly from raw experimental coordinates would show how much the shared post-prep source affects absolute static performance. It would also be useful to evaluate stricter family-aware splits and to test the same representation choices on unbound or predicted structures, where practical affinity prediction is often harder.

\section{Conclusion}

On this benchmark, a full static residue graph was the strongest baseline for protein--protein affinity prediction. In the 52-model comparison, that baseline remained stronger than the short-MD summary variants. In this implementation, the trajectories were too short to add much new affinity signal, and the coarse MD summaries then made that weak signal difficult to use reliably.
