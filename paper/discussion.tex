\chapter{Discussion and Conclusions}
\label{ch:discussion}

\section{Principal findings}

The final confirmatory matrix leads to one strong positive conclusion and one strong negative conclusion. The positive conclusion is that the full residue graph is consistently more informative than the interface-only graph for the present problem setup. The negative conclusion is that the present molecular-dynamics-derived feature encoding does not provide a robust held-out advantage over the static representation. These conclusions are not based on one favourable split, but on a 52-configuration matrix evaluated over 520 total outer fits on a matched 1437-complex subset.

The strongest overall model was the direct supervised baseline on the full static graph, \texttt{full\_base\_S}, with mean test RMSE 2.3379 and mean test Pearson correlation 0.5064. The best variational model, \texttt{full\_S\_semi\_shared\_static\_z32}, was weaker on both metrics. The best dynamic variational model, \texttt{full\_SD\_semi\_shared\_static\_z8}, came close to the best static variational model, but did not surpass the full static baseline and did not establish a stable matrix-wide advantage for the dynamic feature set. These are not the most optimistic results one might have hoped for at the start of the project, but they are precise, interpretable, and scientifically useful.

\section{Why the full graph outperformed the interface graph}

The superiority of the full graph is one of the most stable results in the thesis. Every matched variational comparison favored the full representation, and the same direction held for the supervised baselines. The simplest explanation is that the current interface construction imposes too strong a bottleneck. In the final retained set, full graphs contained on average 495.9 nodes and 10\,697.2 edges, whereas interface graphs contained on average 127.3 nodes and 2106.3 edges. More importantly, 1395 of the 1437 interface graphs, or 97.1\%, reached the hard cap of 128 nodes. In most cases, the interface view was therefore not merely focusing on a local region; it was truncating it.

This result does not show that interface modelling is inherently weak. It shows that the specific 128-node interface patch used here is too restrictive to compete with the full graph on the matched PPB-Affinity subset. A stronger interface result would likely require either a larger patch budget or a less rigid way of selecting which residues remain in view.

\section{Why the present dynamic features did not help}

The dynamic result requires narrower interpretation than the full-versus-interface result. The data do not support the claim that dynamics are unimportant for protein--protein affinity. They support the claim that the particular dynamic representation used here does not add a robust held-out benefit over the static graph.

Several features of the implementation make that outcome plausible. The saved trajectory per complex is short, the dynamic channels are heavily summarized, and temporal order is discarded. Hydration is reduced to mean and standard deviation of nearby water counts, flexibility to C$\alpha$ RMSF, torsional state to mean $\sin/\cos$ values, and edge dynamics to contact-frequency and distance moments on a fixed edge set. The force channels are also approximate protein-only inter-chain proxies rather than exact force decompositions from the original solvated simulation context. Finally, the preparation pipeline makes several simplifying choices that matter for interpretation: missing residues are not rebuilt, non-protein components are removed before simulation, trajectories include equilibration frames, pH defaults to 7.0, and temperature defaults to 298.15 K when unavailable.

Taken together, these choices describe a coarse dynamic summary layer rather than a rich temporal model. The most defensible conclusion is therefore that the current dynamic encoding is not yet a better representation than the static one, not that dynamics themselves are irrelevant.

\section{Why the direct supervised baseline was strongest}

The direct supervised baseline outperformed the best variational model by a modest but consistent margin. This outcome is plausible. The supervised baseline devotes all of its capacity to predicting affinity, whereas the GraphVAE must balance reconstruction, latent regularization, and optional supervision. The variational model is therefore solving a harder optimization problem with more ways to spend capacity on objectives that are only indirectly aligned with held-out affinity prediction.

The results suggest that this trade-off is not favourable at the present data scale. On the matched 1437-complex subset, the full static baseline can exploit the graph representation directly and efficiently. The GraphVAE, by contrast, learns useful latent spaces, but its best latent representation still does not outperform the direct predictive model when judged on held-out RMSE and correlation. This does not mean the GraphVAE is without value. It still offers an interpretable latent representation, a principled reconstruction objective, and a platform for future representation-learning experiments. It does mean, however, that for the present thesis question, the simpler predictive model is the stronger empirical answer.

\section{What the train--validation--test behaviour shows}

The overfitting patterns help distinguish genuine representation weakness from a mere lack of model capacity. The unsupervised GraphVAE families exhibit small train--test gaps, but this is largely because they underfit the affinity target even on their training sets. The semi-supervised families reveal a clearer story. In particular, the semi-supervised $SD$ families fit the training data more strongly than the corresponding $S$ families, yet do not improve held-out performance. This is exactly the pattern one would expect when a feature set increases model flexibility more than it increases robust signal.

The most informative comparison is between the full semi-supervised static and full semi-supervised dynamic families. The dynamic family achieves higher mean training correlation, but lower mean test correlation and a substantially larger train--test RMSE gap. The same pattern is even stronger for the interface semi-supervised dynamic family. This is why the overfitting story is central to interpreting the dynamic results: the extra channels increase training-set fit more than they improve held-out prediction.

\section{Limitations and threats to validity}

Several limitations should be stated explicitly. The first is dataset coverage. Although the curated PPB-Affinity subset contained 2818 complexes, the final confirmatory analysis was restricted to a matched retained subset of 1437 complexes after molecular-dynamics processing, force-feature compatibility checks, and interface-view materialization. The results are therefore not estimates on the entire curated dataset, but on the subset that the full active pipeline could process consistently.

The second limitation is physical fidelity. Missing residues are not rebuilt, heterogens are removed, all simulations use protein-only prepared systems in explicit solvent, and some experimental metadata are missing. The dynamic descriptors are therefore derived from physically meaningful but simplified systems. The third limitation is representational fidelity. The graph topology is static, the interface patch is heavily capped, the trajectories are short, and the dynamic descriptors are summary statistics rather than learned temporal representations. The fourth limitation is evaluation scope. Although the repeated outer resampling design is much stronger than a single split, it is still an internal validation on one dataset family rather than an external benchmark on an entirely different source of complexes and affinity measurements.

These limitations do not invalidate the thesis conclusions. In fact, the main conclusions are carefully phrased so that they remain valid under these limitations. What they do restrict is the scope of interpretation. The thesis provides evidence about a specific and fully described MD-to-graph pipeline. It does not provide the final word on whether dynamics can help protein--protein affinity prediction in general.

\section{Future work}

The most immediate improvement would be to revise the dynamic feature construction rather than to abandon dynamics altogether. Excluding equilibration frames from the feature summaries would be a straightforward correction. Longer production trajectories would make the dynamic summaries less dominated by local relaxation. A richer representation could also retain temporal structure instead of collapsing trajectories to means and standard deviations.

On the graph side, the interface result suggests that the current patching strategy is too restrictive. A larger interface budget, a softer interface-selection strategy, or a hierarchical model that combines local and global context would all be reasonable next tests. On the modelling side, the direct supervised baseline sets a demanding benchmark. Any future representation-learning model should aim either to beat this baseline directly or to provide a secondary benefit that justifies its additional complexity.

\section{Conclusion}

The thesis set out to test whether short explicit-solvent molecular dynamics could improve residue-graph prediction of protein--protein binding affinity on PPB-Affinity-derived complexes. The final answer is precise. For the implemented pipeline, the full residue graph is better than the interface patch, the current dynamic feature set does not improve robust held-out performance over the static graph, and the strongest overall model is a direct supervised baseline on the full static graph. These findings are supported by a repeated outer held-out evaluation rather than by a single favourable split.

This is a useful outcome. It shows that careful resampling can overturn optimistic single-split impressions and that strong static baselines remain difficult to beat. Just as importantly, it leaves a clear path forward. The thesis does not show that dynamics are irrelevant. It shows that they must be represented more carefully than they were here if they are to improve affinity prediction in a reliable way.
