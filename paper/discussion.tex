\chapter{Discussion and Conclusions}
\label{ch:discussion}

The four research questions from Chapter~\ref{ch:intro} can now be answered directly.

First, the full residue graph was better than the interface-restricted representation. The full view won all 24 matched GraphVAE comparisons and both supervised baseline comparisons. For this dataset and these models, restricting the representation to a local interface neighbourhood removed useful information.

Second, the molecular-dynamics-derived summaries did not improve prediction over the static feature set. This conclusion is specific to the summaries tested here: hydration, C$\alpha$ RMSF, torsion summaries, edge-contact frequencies, distance summaries, and approximate inter-chain force proxies attached to a static residue graph. Under that representation, the static models were stronger than the corresponding static-plus-dynamic models.

Third, the masked GraphVAE did not outperform the direct supervised regressor. The best overall model was the full static baseline, while the best GraphVAE was the full static semi-supervised model with latent dimension 32. The variational model still learned predictive representations, but masked reconstruction and latent regularisation did not produce the strongest held-out predictor.

Fourth, the main conclusions were stable under repeated held-out evaluation. The thesis does not rest on a single split. The same design choices remained favourable across 10 outer fits, and the family-level summaries tell the same story as the leading models.

Subtype heterogeneity also remained visible in the full comparison. On pooled outer-test predictions, the best overall model (\texttt{full\_base\_S}) gave Pearson $r=0.292$ on antibody--antigen, $r=0.430$ on the broad other class, and $r=0.466$ on TCR-pMHC. The full static baseline remained the strongest of the main models in all three interaction classes.

The main limitations are the short trajectory length, the static graph topology, the use of time-aggregated dynamic features rather than an explicitly temporal model, and evaluation on one dataset family. A practical limitation is dataset scale: the curated table contained 2818 complexes, but the full MD and repeated-resampling study was run on a smaller 1437-complex analysis subset. PPB-Affinity reported better benchmark performance as the training fraction increased, so this restricted study size matters \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}. A second limitation is the split itself. The resampling was repeated, but it was not family-disjoint. Earlier protein--protein work explicitly removed homologous complexes to avoid optimistic evaluation, and protein--ligand studies have shown that structural overlap between training and test sets can inflate reported affinity-prediction performance \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction,Graber2025DataBias}. Natural extensions are therefore larger MD-backed study sets, longer simulations, models that operate directly on temporal graph sequences, and stricter split definitions.

Within this study, short explicit-solvent molecular-dynamics summaries did not improve protein--protein binding affinity prediction over a static residue graph, and the strongest overall model was the direct supervised baseline on the full static graph.
