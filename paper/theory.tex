\chapter{Background and Theoretical Context}
\label{ch:theory}

\section{Affinity as the target quantity}

The quantitative target in this thesis is the standard Gibbs free energy of binding, $\Delta G$. For a complex written schematically as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{P}_1 + \mathrm{P}_2 \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{P}_1\mathrm{P}_2,
\end{equation}
the equilibrium dissociation constant is
\begin{equation}
K_D = \frac{[\mathrm{P}_1][\mathrm{P}_2]}{[\mathrm{P}_1\mathrm{P}_2]},
\end{equation}
and the corresponding free energy is
\begin{equation}
\Delta G = RT \ln K_D.
\end{equation}
This matters because it gives a single continuous regression target, but it also highlights the central difficulty of the problem: affinity is a thermodynamic quantity, whereas the model sees only structure-derived features from a finite set of observed conformations \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}.

\section{Molecular dynamics as a source of descriptors}

In classical molecular dynamics, atomic coordinates evolve under Newton's equations of motion,
\begin{equation}
m_i \frac{d^2 \mathbf{r}_i}{dt^2} = - \nabla_{\mathbf{r}_i} U(\mathbf{r}_1,\dots,\mathbf{r}_N),
\end{equation}
where $U$ is the force-field potential energy. In this thesis, molecular dynamics is used descriptively rather than as a direct free-energy method. The goal is to extract trajectory-derived summaries such as hydration, flexibility, and contact persistence and test whether they improve a downstream affinity predictor. This is plausible because proteins are dynamic rather than rigid objects, but the usefulness of such descriptors depends on simulation length, feature design, and model integration \citep{HenzlerWildman2007Dynamic}.

\section{Residue graphs and message passing}

A protein complex can be represented as a graph $G=(V,E)$ in which residues form the nodes and selected residue pairs form the edges. This representation is a natural compromise between atom-level detail and computational tractability, and it aligns well with residue-level descriptors such as solvent exposure, buried surface area, residue depth, and physicochemical identity \citep{Crocioni2024DeepRank2}. In the present thesis, the graph topology is static, while some node and edge attributes summarise time-dependent behaviour from molecular dynamics.

Graph neural networks learn on such inputs by message passing. In a generic layer,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_i^{(\ell+1)}
=
\phi^{(\ell)}
\left(
\mathbf{h}_i^{(\ell)},
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)}
\psi^{(\ell)}\left(\mathbf{h}_i^{(\ell)}, \mathbf{h}_j^{(\ell)}, \mathbf{e}_{ij}\right)
\right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{h}_i^{(\ell)}$ is the node state, $\mathbf{e}_{ij}$ is the edge feature vector, and $\psi^{(\ell)}$ and $\phi^{(\ell)}$ are learnable message and update functions. After several layers, node states are pooled into a graph embedding for downstream prediction. The models used in this thesis employ GINE-style message passing, which is edge-aware and therefore appropriate when both static and dynamic edge features are present \citep{Xu2019GIN,Hu2020Pretraining}.

\section{Variational graph representation learning}

The GraphVAE used in this thesis is a latent-variable model that combines graph encoding, masked reconstruction, and latent regularisation. In the standard VAE formulation, training optimises a variational lower bound,
\begin{equation}
\log p_{\theta}(\mathbf{x})
\ge
\mathbb{E}_{q_{\phi}(\mathbf{z}\mid \mathbf{x})}
\left[
\log p_{\theta}(\mathbf{x}\mid \mathbf{z})
\right]
-
D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_{\phi}(\mathbf{z}\mid \mathbf{x}) \,\|\, p(\mathbf{z})\right),
\end{equation}
where the reconstruction term encourages the latent variable to retain useful information about the input and the Kullback--Leibler term keeps the latent distribution close to a simple prior \citep{Kingma2014VAE}. In this thesis, the model is used as a representation learner rather than as a full generative model of protein graphs. The encoder maps a residue graph to a graph-level latent distribution, and the decoder reconstructs selected masked node and edge features. A supervised affinity head can optionally be attached to the latent mean.

\section{Why repeated held-out evaluation matters}

When many closely related models are compared, a single train/validation/test split can give a misleading impression of relative performance \citep{Kohavi1995CV}. For that reason, the final conclusions in this thesis are based on repeated outer held-out evaluation rather than on a one-off split. The point of the final evaluation matrix is not only to identify the strongest configuration, but to compare design choices such as full versus interface graphs, static versus dynamic features, and variational versus direct supervised training under a more stable evaluation protocol.
