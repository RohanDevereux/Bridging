\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

\section{Problem and motivation}

Protein--protein interactions are biologically central and therapeutically important \citep{Modell2016PPIs}. For many applications, the relevant question is not only whether two proteins bind, but how strongly they bind. The natural quantitative target is the binding free energy, or equivalently the dissociation constant $K_D$. Predicting this quantity from structure is appealing because it is experimentally measurable and relevant to biomolecular design \citep{Guo2022PPBAReview,Liu2024PPBAffinity}. It is also difficult. A deposited structure is only one conformation, while the measured affinity reflects a thermodynamic average over many molecular states \citep{Guo2022PPBAReview,Liu2024PPBAffinity}.

This difficulty motivates the central question of the thesis: can short explicit-solvent molecular-dynamics simulations provide useful information beyond a carefully constructed static residue graph? The question is narrow on purpose. The thesis does not attempt rigorous free-energy calculation. It asks whether a practical MD-to-graph pipeline improves held-out prediction on a modern protein--protein affinity dataset.

\section{Research questions and scope}

The work is centred on one pipeline. Starting from a curated broad subset of PPB-Affinity, each complex is prepared for molecular dynamics, simulated in explicit solvent, converted to a residue graph with DeepRank2, augmented with trajectory-derived descriptors, and then passed to either a masked GraphVAE or a direct supervised baseline. The final evaluation is restricted to a matched 1437-complex subset for which both full and interface graph views can be materialized successfully.

Within that scope, the thesis asks four concrete questions. First, is a full residue graph more predictive than an interface-only graph? Second, do molecular-dynamics-derived features improve prediction relative to static graph features alone? Third, does a masked GraphVAE provide a better representation than a direct supervised graph regressor when both models receive the same inputs? Fourth, what do repeated held-out evaluations reveal about underfitting, overfitting, and the reliability of conclusions drawn from a single split?

\section{What the thesis contributes}

The main contribution is a fully specified and reproducible comparison. The thesis evaluates full versus interface graphs, static versus static-plus-dynamic features, GraphVAE versus direct supervised regression, and unsupervised versus semi-supervised variational training under repeated outer resampling rather than a single favourable split. The goal is not to claim that dynamics must help. The goal is to determine, as cleanly as possible, what the implemented pipeline actually supports.

\section{Thesis structure}

Chapter~\ref{ch:litreview} places the work in the context of protein--protein affinity prediction, molecular simulation, and learned structural representations. Chapter~\ref{ch:theory} introduces the technical ideas needed for the rest of the thesis. Chapter~\ref{chap:methods} describes the implemented pipeline in replication-level detail. Chapter~\ref{ch:results} reports the final model matrix. Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} interprets the results, states the main limitations, and gives the final conclusions. The appendices provide additional technical background, archived project branches, and a map from the written thesis to the public repository.
