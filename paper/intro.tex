\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

Protein--protein binding affinity prediction asks how strongly two proteins bind. In benchmark datasets for this task, the target is a thermodynamic quantity such as dissociation constant $K_D$ or binding free energy $\Delta G$, and the input is usually a single three-dimensional complex structure \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}. Accurate affinity prediction is important for protein design, therapeutic targeting, and drug discovery \citep{Guo2022PPBAReview,Modell2016PPIs}. Experimental measurements provide the ground truth, but they are too slow and expensive for large exploratory searches. Physics-based free-energy calculations aim at the same quantity, but the more faithful versions are also expensive \citep{Cournia2017RBFE,Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview}. Many practical machine-learning predictors therefore estimate affinity quickly from structural input alone.

One difficulty is that binding affinity is an ensemble property. It depends on the range of shapes and relative arrangements the proteins sample, not just on one frozen structure. Proteins are dynamic, and many protein pairs also reorganise at the interface as they bind \citep{HenzlerWildman2007Dynamic,Kastritis2011Benchmark}, so a single bound complex can miss relevant information. Fast structure-based predictors therefore have to decide how much of the complex to represent. Some recent protein--protein models focus tightly on the interface, whereas others use whole-complex or hybrid representations \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity,Moal2011AffinityPrediction,RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity,Xie2025PPIGraphomer}. They also have to decide whether one bound structure is enough. Protein--ligand work has explored the use of MD beyond a single bound structure more extensively \citep{Min2024Dynaformer,Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}. Residue graphs make it possible to examine both questions in the same protein--protein setting. In this representation, amino-acid residues become nodes and residue--residue proximity or contact defines edges.

A curated wild-type, that is, non-mutant, subset of PPB-Affinity \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity} provides the data. The starting curated table contained 2818 complexes, from which a matched 1437-complex subset was retained. Each retained complex went through the same preparation and short-MD procedure. DeepRank2 converted the prepared protein structure into a residue graph, and trajectory-derived summaries supplied the dynamic features. Affinity was then predicted with two model families. One path trained a masked GraphVAE, computed graph-level latent means, and predicted affinity from those latents with ridge regression. The other trained a direct supervised graph regressor on the same residue graph. The shared setup lets graph scope, feature mode, and model family be compared on equal terms.

Four questions guide the analysis: whether a full residue graph is better than an interface-restricted representation; whether MD-derived summaries improve prediction over static graph features alone; whether a masked GraphVAE produces a better predictive representation than a direct supervised graph regressor with the same encoder architecture; and how stable those answers remain under repeated held-out evaluation.

Chapter~\ref{ch:litreview} frames those questions through benchmark design, representation scope, and dynamics. Chapter~\ref{chap:methods} describes the methods. Chapter~\ref{ch:results} answers the four questions with the 52-model comparison. Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} interprets those answers and sets out the main limitations and next steps. The appendices cover archived approaches, the complete model table, and the repository layout.
