\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:litreview}

\section{Protein--protein benchmark}

Protein--ligand benchmarking has had a shared structural resource since PDBbind \citep{Wang2004PDBbind}. Curated protein--protein datasets for absolute affinity remained much smaller for many years \citep{Kastritis2011Benchmark}. Protein--protein affinity prediction has therefore developed with less data and with more variation between datasets. PPB-Affinity was introduced to improve that situation by building a larger benchmark specifically for direct protein--protein affinity prediction \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}. Reviews of the field repeatedly point to three linked difficulties: limited affinity data, mixed interaction classes, and proteins changing shape as they bind \citep{Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview,Guo2022PPBAReview}. Those difficulties affect both modelling and evaluation. They limit how confidently models can be compared, and they weaken the assumption that one bound structure fully captures binding strength.

This makes single-structure scoring especially restrictive. In the Kastritis benchmark, only a minority of complexes were close to rigid-body association, and large or moderate interface rearrangements were common \citep{Kastritis2011Benchmark}. Many protein pairs bind through bending, shifting, or interface reorganisation instead of simple rigid docking. A useful predictor has to cope with both shape changes within a complex and structural differences across complexes.

PPB-Affinity also shows how heterogeneous this prediction task is. Its benchmark model reached Pearson $r=0.701$ on the full test set, but subgroup performance was weaker for both antibody--antigen and T-cell receptor--peptide major histocompatibility complex (TCR-pMHC) complexes. The authors linked the TCR-pMHC difficulty to strong interface similarity within that class and noted that both subgroups contain a higher proportion of mutant samples than the full dataset \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}. Later antibody-specific models reach a similar conclusion. Yang et al.\ argued that general protein--protein affinity predictors often transfer poorly to antibody--antigen complexes and proposed specialised regressors instead \citep{Yang2023AntibodyAffinity}. Subgroup performance is a central issue in this field. Different interaction classes combine different affinity ranges, interface similarities, and mutation patterns, so they can reward different representations.

\section{Protein--protein methods}

Umbrella sampling and alchemical free-energy methods aim more directly at binding free energy, but they are expensive and sensitive to protocol design \citep{Torrie1977Umbrella,Cournia2017RBFE}. Most of that literature is protein--ligand focused. For protein--protein systems, the same calculations are usually harder because the complexes are larger and more flexible \citep{Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview}. MM/PBSA and MM/GBSA are cheaper endpoint approximations \citep{Massova2000MMPBSA}, but they still depend strongly on setup choices and require careful interpretation. At the other end of the spectrum, fast empirical models use compact structural summaries. PRODIGY predicts affinity from interface contact counts and non-interacting surface descriptors \citep{Vangone2015Contacts}, while Moal et al.\ used engineered whole-complex descriptors \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction}.

Current learned protein--protein models make different choices about how much structure to keep and how tightly to focus on the interface. PRODIGY and the PPB-Affinity benchmark model are explicitly interface-focused \citep{Vangone2015Contacts,Liu2024PPBAffinity}. Moal et al.\ and PPI-Affinity instead build descriptor sets from the complex as a whole \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction,RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity}. PPI-Graphomer sits between those extremes: it builds an interface-aware module, but the final predictor combines that interface representation with complete sequence and structure information \citep{Xie2025PPIGraphomer}. Representation scope is one of the main design choices across these models. Interface-focused methods keep the input compact and concentrate on the contact region. Whole-complex and hybrid methods retain broader structural context that may still influence affinity. No single scope choice has won across the literature. Matched scope comparisons are useful for that reason.

\section{Protein--ligand comparison}

Protein--ligand affinity prediction differs from protein--protein affinity prediction in ways that matter here. In protein--ligand work, the ligand is usually much smaller, the interaction is more tightly localised to one binding pocket, and larger shared resources such as PDBbind have been available for much longer \citep{Wang2004PDBbind}. Protein--protein systems are larger, more heterogeneous, and often require the model to decide how much of two extended molecular surfaces should be retained \citep{Guo2022PPBAReview,Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview}. In protein--ligand prediction, pocket-restricted voxel or graph representations are often a natural choice. In protein--protein prediction, the same design can discard context that still contributes to binding strength.

Protein--ligand work suggests two useful lessons. Pafnucy showed that a fixed local 3D representation could support successful affinity prediction when the binding problem is tightly localised \citep{Stepniewska2018Pafnucy}. More recent work has also used MD. Dynaformer argues that static structures can miss affinity-relevant information present in conformational ensembles, while Timenucy and Videonucy learn from frame sequences instead of simple trajectory averages \citep{Min2024Dynaformer,Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}. Together these papers suggest that local representations can work well when the interaction is strongly localised, and that dynamic information is more likely to help when the model keeps richer trajectory information than a small set of summary statistics.

\section{Generalisation and evaluation}

Reported accuracy in this area depends strongly on how train and test examples are separated. PPI-Affinity reported that, in its own re-evaluation, PRODIGY reached $r=0.74$ on a 79-complex benchmark subset but only $r=0.31$ on a separate 90-complex hold-out set \citep{RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity}. Moal et al.\ had already tried to reduce the same problem by removing homologous complexes, so evaluation stayed clear of near-duplicate training examples \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction}. In protein--ligand scoring, Graber et al.\ later showed that train--test leakage between PDBbind and CASF had inflated benchmark performance, and that stricter splits caused clear drops for previously strong models \citep{Graber2025DataBias}. Split design shapes the scientific interpretation. Reported gains can reflect a stronger model, an easier split, or both. Evaluation belongs to the scientific question itself, not just to how results are reported.

\section{Variational models}

Variational autoencoders were introduced as latent-variable models that combine reconstruction with a regularised latent distribution \citep{Kingma2014VAE}. Variational graph autoencoders brought the same idea to graph data \citep{Kipf2016VGAE}, and later GraphVAE-style models made graph generation more explicit \citep{Simonovsky2018GraphVAE}. In molecular machine learning, related latent-space VAEs were already being used successfully for small molecules \citep{GomezBombarelli2018MolecularVAE}. The use here is narrower than full graph generation. The GraphVAE is used to learn a compact residue-graph representation and then test whether that representation carries useful affinity signal.

Simulation work also supports this choice. Tian et al.\ showed that a VAE trained on protein MD trajectories could learn a latent space that captured physically plausible structural variation \citep{Tian2021ProteinVAE}. That result suggests that a latent model may be useful when structure and MD features are high-dimensional and only part of that information is relevant to binding strength.

\section{Field picture}

Recent protein--ligand work has pushed furthest on combining MD with learned structural representations, including models that use ensemble information or ordered trajectory frames instead of a single static complex \citep{Min2024Dynaformer,Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}. That literature is older, larger, and methodologically further along on MD-based learning than the corresponding protein--protein literature. Protein--protein affinity prediction is only recently reaching the scale where comparable questions can be asked on larger curated datasets such as PPB-Affinity \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}. Current work still disagrees on evaluation, representation scope, and dynamic modelling. Scores depend strongly on dataset construction and split design \citep{Guo2022PPBAReview,RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity,Graber2025DataBias}. Interface-focused models keep a compact local signal, while whole-complex and hybrid models retain structural context that may still matter for affinity \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity,RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity,Xie2025PPIGraphomer}. Dynamic modelling is further developed in protein--ligand prediction than in protein--protein prediction, partly because protein--protein systems are larger, more heterogeneous, and harder to simulate and benchmark at scale \citep{Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview,Min2024Dynaformer,Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}. Short explicit-solvent MD summaries on matched whole-complex and interface-restricted residue graphs bring that more mature MD question into a protein--protein benchmark where the problem is harder and the evidence base is newer.
