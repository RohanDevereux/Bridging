\section{Protein--protein benchmark}

Protein--ligand benchmarking has had a shared structural resource since PDBbind \citep{Wang2004PDBbind}, whereas curated protein--protein datasets for absolute affinity remained much smaller for many years \citep{Kastritis2011Benchmark}. Protein--protein affinity prediction has therefore developed with less data and with more variation between datasets. PPB-Affinity was introduced to improve that situation by building a larger benchmark specifically for direct protein--protein affinity prediction \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}. Reviews of the field repeatedly point to three practical difficulties: limited affinity data, mixed interaction classes, and proteins changing shape as they bind \citep{Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview,Guo2022PPBAReview}. Protein--protein affinity prediction is hard because it combines limited data, mixed interaction classes, and structural flexibility.

This makes single-structure scoring especially restrictive. In the Kastritis benchmark, only a minority of complexes were close to rigid-body association, and large or moderate interface rearrangements were common \citep{Kastritis2011Benchmark}. Many protein pairs bind through bending, shifting, or interface reorganisation instead of simple rigid docking. A useful predictor has to cope with both shape changes within a complex and structural differences across complexes.

PPB-Affinity also shows how heterogeneous this prediction task is. Its benchmark model reached Pearson $r=0.701$ on the full test set, but subgroup performance was weaker for both antibody--antigen and T-cell receptor--peptide major histocompatibility complex (TCR-pMHC) complexes. The authors linked the TCR-pMHC difficulty to strong interface similarity within that class and noted that both subgroups contain a higher proportion of mutant samples than the full dataset \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}. Later antibody-specific models make the same point from another angle: Yang et al.\ argued that general protein--protein affinity predictors often transfer poorly to antibody--antigen complexes and proposed specialised regressors instead \citep{Yang2023AntibodyAffinity}. Subgroup performance is a central issue in this field. Different interaction classes combine different affinity ranges, interface similarities, and mutation patterns, so they can reward different representations.

\section{Protein--protein methods}

Umbrella sampling and alchemical free-energy methods aim more directly at binding free energy, but they are expensive and sensitive to protocol design \citep{Torrie1977Umbrella,Cournia2017RBFE}. Most of that literature is protein--ligand focused. For protein--protein systems, the same calculations are usually harder because the complexes are larger and more flexible \citep{Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview}. MM/PBSA and MM/GBSA are cheaper endpoint approximations \citep{Massova2000MMPBSA}, but they still depend strongly on setup choices and require careful interpretation. At the other end of the spectrum, fast empirical models use compact structural summaries. PRODIGY predicts affinity from interface contact counts and non-interacting surface descriptors \citep{Vangone2015Contacts}, while Moal et al.\ used engineered whole-complex descriptors \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction}.

Current learned protein--protein models make different choices about how much structure to keep and how tightly to focus on the interface. PRODIGY and the PPB-Affinity benchmark model are explicitly interface-focused \citep{Vangone2015Contacts,Liu2024PPBAffinity}. Moal et al.\ and PPI-Affinity instead build descriptor sets from the complex as a whole rather than from a fixed interface crop \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction,RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity}. PPI-Graphomer sits between those extremes: it builds an interface-aware module, but the final predictor combines that interface representation with complete sequence and structure information \citep{Xie2025PPIGraphomer}. Scope is one of the main design choices across these models. Interface-focused methods keep a cleaner local signal, while whole-complex and hybrid methods retain more structural context. The final comparison tests that choice directly under matched training and evaluation conditions.

\section{Protein--ligand comparison}

Protein--ligand affinity prediction differs from protein--protein affinity prediction in ways that matter here. In protein--ligand work, the ligand is usually much smaller, the interaction is more tightly localised to one binding pocket, and larger shared resources such as PDBbind have been available for much longer \citep{Wang2004PDBbind}. Protein--protein systems are larger, more heterogeneous, and often require the model to decide how much of two extended molecular surfaces should be retained \citep{Guo2022PPBAReview,Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview}. In protein--ligand prediction, pocket-restricted voxel or graph representations are often a natural choice. In protein--protein prediction, the same design can discard context that still contributes to binding strength.

The protein--ligand literature still points to useful ideas. Pafnucy showed that a fixed local 3D representation could support successful affinity prediction when the binding problem is tightly localised \citep{Stepniewska2018Pafnucy}. More recent work has also used MD. Dynaformer argues that static structures can miss affinity-relevant information present in conformational ensembles, while Timenucy and Videonucy learn from frame sequences rather than from simple trajectory averages \citep{Min2024Dynaformer,Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}. Together these papers suggest two ideas that matter here: local structural representations can work well when the interaction is strongly localised, and dynamic information is more likely to help when the model keeps richer trajectory information than a small set of summary statistics.

\section{Generalisation and evaluation}

Reported accuracy in this area depends strongly on how train and test examples are separated. PPI-Affinity reported that, in its own re-evaluation, PRODIGY reached $r=0.74$ on a 79-complex benchmark subset but only $r=0.31$ on a separate 90-complex hold-out set \citep{RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity}. Moal et al.\ had already tried to reduce the same problem by removing homologous complexes, so evaluation stayed clear of near-duplicate training examples \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction}. In protein--ligand scoring, Graber et al.\ later showed that train--test leakage between PDBbind and CASF had inflated benchmark performance, and that stricter splits caused clear drops for previously strong models \citep{Graber2025DataBias}. Split design shapes the scientific interpretation: reported gains can come from a stronger model, an easier split, or both.

\section{Variational models}

Variational autoencoders were introduced as latent-variable models that combine reconstruction with a regularised latent distribution \citep{Kingma2014VAE}. Variational graph autoencoders brought the same idea to graph data \citep{Kipf2016VGAE}, and later GraphVAE-style models made graph generation more explicit \citep{Simonovsky2018GraphVAE}. In molecular machine learning, related latent-space VAEs were already being used successfully for small molecules \citep{GomezBombarelli2018MolecularVAE}. Here the point is narrower than full graph generation: the GraphVAE is used to learn a compact residue-graph representation and then test whether that representation carries useful affinity signal.

Simulation work also supports this choice. Tian et al.\ showed that a VAE trained on protein MD trajectories could learn a latent space that captured physically plausible structural variation \citep{Tian2021ProteinVAE}. That result suggests that a latent model may be useful when structure and MD features are high-dimensional and only part of that information is relevant to binding strength.

\section{Study position}

The final design sits between fast single-structure scoring and much heavier free-energy calculation \citep{Cournia2017RBFE,Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview}. It uses short explicit-solvent MD to build extra residue-graph features and compares whole-complex and interface-restricted residue graphs with and without those dynamic summaries. The protein--ligand literature motivates the dynamic side of that design, while the protein--protein literature motivates the comparison between local and whole-complex representations \citep{Min2024Dynaformer,Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}.
