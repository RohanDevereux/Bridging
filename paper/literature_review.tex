\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:litreview}

\section{Protein--protein benchmark and evaluation}

Protein--ligand affinity prediction has had a shared structural resource since PDBbind \citep{Wang2004PDBbind}. Curated protein--protein datasets for absolute affinity remained much smaller for many years \citep{Kastritis2011Benchmark}. Protein--protein affinity prediction therefore developed with less data and with more variation between datasets. PPB-Affinity was introduced to improve that situation by building a larger benchmark for direct protein--protein affinity prediction \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}. Reviews of the area point to the same practical difficulties: affinity data are limited, interaction classes are mixed, and protein pairs often change shape as they bind \citep{Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview,Guo2022PPBAReview}. Those problems affect evaluation as well as modelling.

Single-structure scoring is restrictive in this setting. In the Kastritis benchmark, only a minority of complexes were close to rigid-body association, and large or moderate interface rearrangements were common \citep{Kastritis2011Benchmark}. Many protein pairs bind through bending, shifting, or local reorganisation instead of simple rigid docking. A useful predictor therefore has to cope with both shape changes within one complex and structural differences across complexes.

Benchmark design also shapes the apparent difficulty of the task because the benchmark is heterogeneous. PPB-Affinity reported Pearson $r=0.701$ for its benchmark model on the full test set, but subgroup performance was weaker for both antibody--antigen and T-cell receptor--peptide major histocompatibility complex (TCR-pMHC) complexes \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}. The same paper linked the TCR-pMHC difficulty to strong interface similarity within that class and noted that both subgroups contain a higher proportion of mutant samples than the full dataset. Yang et al.\ reached a similar conclusion for antibody--antigen prediction and proposed specialised regressors for that class \citep{Yang2023AntibodyAffinity}. Different interaction classes combine different affinity ranges, interface similarities, and mutation patterns, so pooled benchmark averages can hide real differences in difficulty.

Split design changes the apparent performance of a predictor. PPI-Affinity reported that PRODIGY reached $r=0.74$ on a 79-complex benchmark subset but only $r=0.31$ on a separate 90-complex hold-out set \citep{RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity}. Moal et al.\ reduced redundancy by removing homologous complexes from evaluation \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction}. In protein--ligand scoring, Graber et al.\ later showed that train--test leakage between PDBbind and CASF had inflated benchmark performance and that stricter splits caused clear drops for previously strong models \citep{Graber2025DataBias}. Evaluation is therefore part of the scientific problem. Reported gains can come from model quality, split difficulty, or both.

\section{Representation scope}

Umbrella sampling and alchemical free-energy methods aim more directly at binding free energy, but they are expensive and sensitive to protocol design \citep{Torrie1977Umbrella,Cournia2017RBFE}. Most of that literature is protein--ligand focused. For protein--protein systems, the same calculations are usually harder because the complexes are larger and more flexible \citep{Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview}. MM/PBSA and MM/GBSA are cheaper endpoint approximations \citep{Massova2000MMPBSA}, but they still depend strongly on setup choices and need careful interpretation. Fast structural predictors sit much further down the cost scale, but they then have to decide how much of the complex to keep.

That scope choice runs through the protein--protein literature. PRODIGY and the PPB-Affinity benchmark model are explicitly interface-focused \citep{Vangone2015Contacts,Liu2024PPBAffinity}. Moal et al.\ and PPI-Affinity instead build descriptor sets from the complex as a whole \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction,RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity}. PPI-Graphomer sits between those extremes: it builds an interface-aware module, but the final predictor combines that interface representation with complete sequence and structure information \citep{Xie2025PPIGraphomer}. Interface-focused models keep the input compact and concentrate on the contact region. Whole-complex and hybrid models retain broader structural context that may still influence affinity. The published literature does not settle that choice cleanly, which makes matched scope comparisons useful.

External or stricter evaluation can lower them sharply. Score context is useful, but direct ranking across papers is limited unless the evaluation setup is closely matched.

\section{Dynamics and the protein--ligand comparison}

Protein--ligand affinity prediction differs from protein--protein affinity prediction in several relevant ways. In protein--ligand work, the ligand is usually much smaller, and the interaction is more tightly localised to one binding pocket. Larger shared resources such as PDBbind have also been available for much longer \citep{Wang2004PDBbind}. Protein--protein systems are larger, more heterogeneous, and often require the model to decide how much of two extended molecular surfaces should be retained \citep{Guo2022PPBAReview,Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview}. In protein--ligand prediction, pocket-restricted voxel or graph representations are often a natural choice. In protein--protein prediction, the same design can discard context that still contributes to binding strength.

Protein--ligand work suggests two useful lessons. Pafnucy showed that a fixed local 3D representation could support successful affinity prediction when the binding problem is tightly localised \citep{Stepniewska2018Pafnucy}. More recent work has also used MD. Dynaformer argues that static structures can miss affinity-relevant information present in conformational ensembles. Timenucy and Videonucy instead learn from frame sequences instead of simple trajectory averages \citep{Min2024Dynaformer,Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}. Together these papers suggest that local representations can work well when the interaction is strongly localised, and that dynamic information is more likely to help when the model keeps richer trajectory information than a small set of summary statistics.

\section{Variational models}

Variational autoencoders were introduced as latent-variable models that combine reconstruction with a regularised latent distribution \citep{Kingma2014VAE}. Variational graph autoencoders brought the same idea to graph data \citep{Kipf2016VGAE}, and later GraphVAE-style models made graph generation more explicit \citep{Simonovsky2018GraphVAE}. In molecular machine learning, related latent-space VAEs were already being used successfully for small molecules \citep{GomezBombarelli2018MolecularVAE}. The use here is narrower than full graph generation. The GraphVAE is used to learn a compact residue-graph representation and then test whether that representation carries useful affinity signal.

Simulation work also supports this choice. Tian et al.\ showed that a VAE trained on protein MD trajectories could learn a latent space that captured physically plausible structural variation \citep{Tian2021ProteinVAE}. That result suggests that a latent model may be useful when structure and MD features are high-dimensional and only part of that information is relevant to binding strength.

\section{Position of this study}

The literature points to three linked questions: evaluation design changes the apparent difficulty of the task; representation scope changes how much structural context a model can use; and dynamics remains open. Protein--ligand work has moved further on MD-informed learning, but protein--protein systems are larger, more heterogeneous, and harder to benchmark at comparable scale \citep{Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview,Min2024Dynaformer,Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}. This study brings those questions together on one protein--protein benchmark by comparing full and interface-restricted residue graphs, static and short-MD summary features, and two model families under one shared evaluation design.
