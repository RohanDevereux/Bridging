\section{Protein--protein affinity prediction as a benchmark problem}

Absolute protein--protein affinity prediction is a smaller and less standardized literature than protein--ligand scoring. Protein--ligand benchmarking has had a shared structural resource since PDBbind \citep{Wang2004PDBbind}, whereas curated protein--protein benchmarks for absolute affinity remained much smaller for many years \citep{Kastritis2011Benchmark}. PPB-Affinity is a recent dataset built specifically for machine-learning work on direct protein--protein affinity prediction at larger scale \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}. Reviews of the field still describe limited labelled data, broad interface heterogeneity, and conformational change as central obstacles \citep{Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview,Guo2022PPBAReview,Liu2024PPBAffinity}.

Single-structure scoring is particularly restrictive for protein--protein complexes. In the Kastritis benchmark, only a minority of complexes were close to rigid-body association, and large or moderate interface rearrangements were common \citep{Kastritis2011Benchmark}. Moal et al. likewise emphasized that flexible complexes and diverse functional classes make the task harder than the narrow enzyme--inhibitor benchmarks used by much of the older literature \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction}. The literature is correspondingly more fragmented than the protein--ligand literature.

PPB-Affinity also makes the subtype heterogeneity explicit. Its benchmark model reached Pearson $r=0.701$ on the full test set, but the subgroup plots were weaker for both antibody--antigen and TCR-pMHC \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}. The paper attributes the TCR-pMHC difficulty to strong interface similarity within that class and notes that both subgroups contain a higher proportion of mutant samples than the full dataset \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}. Subgroup-specific models have appeared for the same reason. Yang et al. developed antibody--antigen-specific affinity regressors and argued that general protein--protein affinity predictors do not transfer to antibody--antigen complexes with comparable accuracy \citep{Yang2023AntibodyAffinity}.

\section{Physics-based, empirical, and learned approaches}

Physics-based affinity estimation remains the reference point. Umbrella sampling and alchemical free-energy methods target the binding free energy more directly, but they are expensive and sensitive to protocol design \citep{Torrie1977Umbrella,Cournia2017RBFE}. MM/PBSA and MM/GBSA are cheaper endpoint approximations and are therefore attractive as practical baselines, but they remain approximate and system-dependent \citep{Massova2000MMPBSA,Genheden2015MMGBSA}. Fast empirical estimators occupy the other extreme. PRODIGY predicts affinity from interfacial contact counts and non-interacting surface descriptors \citep{Vangone2015Contacts}, while descriptor-based regressors such as the model of Moal et al.\ use engineered structural summaries of the complex \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction}.

More recent work has moved toward learned structural representations. In protein--protein affinity prediction, the common pattern is still interface-centered learning: hand-designed interface descriptors, interface-derived descriptor vectors, or interface-focused geometric deep learning \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity,RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity,Xie2025PPIGraphomer}. In protein--ligand affinity prediction, the literature is broader and older, spanning binding-site voxel CNNs such as Pafnucy \citep{Stepniewska2018Pafnucy} and MD-aware spatio-temporal models such as Timenucy and Videonucy \citep{Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}.

\section{Generalization and evaluation}

External-test-set behavior already exposed weak generalization in this literature. PPI-Affinity reported that PRODIGY fell from $r=0.74$ on the original benchmark of Vangone and Bonvin to $r=0.31$ on a separate 90-complex hold-out set, and interpreted that drop as evidence of overfitting to the earlier benchmark \citep{RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity}. Moal et al.\ had already tried to guard against the same problem by removing homologous complexes so that leave-one-out evaluation would not benefit from near-duplicate training examples \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction}. In protein--ligand scoring, Graber et al.\ later showed directly that structural overlap between PDBbind and CASF had inflated apparent benchmark performance, and that retraining on leakage-reduced splits caused marked performance drops for previously strong models \citep{Graber2025DataBias}. External or family-disjoint evaluation is necessary when reported accuracy is interpreted.

\section{Why use a variational model}

Variational autoencoders were introduced as latent-variable models that combine reconstruction with a regularized latent distribution \citep{Kingma2014VAE}. Variational graph autoencoders extended the same idea to graph data \citep{Kipf2016VGAE}. That is not yet a standard route for endpoint protein--protein affinity prediction, but it is relevant as a representation-learning strategy when the input is high-dimensional and only part of the signal is likely to be affinity-relevant.

The specific motivation comes from molecular simulation. Tian et al.\ showed that a VAE trained on protein MD trajectories could learn a latent space that generated unsampled but physically plausible conformations and helped explore hidden regions of the conformational landscape \citep{Tian2021ProteinVAE}. The role of the GraphVAE in this thesis is analogous. It is not used as a generic graph generator; it is used to compress residue graphs built from static structure and short MD-derived summaries into a latent representation that can then be tested for affinity prediction.

\section{Position of this thesis}

This thesis sits between fast structure-based scoring and expensive free-energy calculation. It does not attempt rigorous binding free-energy estimation, and it does not rely on a small hand-designed interface score alone. The central question is whether short explicit-solvent MD can improve a residue-graph representation for direct protein--protein affinity prediction, and whether that answer changes with graph view or model family.
