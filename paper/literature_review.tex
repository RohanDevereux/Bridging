\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:litreview}

\section{Protein--protein affinity prediction as a distinct modelling problem}

Protein--protein binding affinity prediction sits between structural bioinformatics, computational chemistry, and machine learning. The target quantity is thermodynamic, but the input available to a learning model is usually a single experimentally observed structure. This creates an immediate mismatch: the deposited complex is one conformation, whereas the measured affinity reflects a free-energy difference between bound and unbound ensembles. Reviews of the field and the PPB-Affinity dataset paper both emphasize that this mismatch, together with limited and heterogeneous labelled data, is a central reason the task remains difficult \citep{Guo2022PPBAReview,Liu2024PPBAffinity}.

The benchmark landscape reflects that difficulty. A large part of the older supervised literature focused on mutation-induced changes in affinity rather than direct prediction of absolute binding affinity for heterogeneous wild-type complexes. SKEMPI 2.0 is the best-known example of this mutation-centred regime \citep{Jankauskaite2019SKEMPI}. Dedicated absolute-affinity resources do exist, including the structure-based benchmark assembled by Kastritis and coworkers, but they are small by modern deep-learning standards \citep{Kastritis2011Benchmark}. PPB-Affinity is therefore an important development because it was assembled specifically for direct protein--protein affinity prediction at larger scale and with explicit ligand/receptor chain annotations \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}.

\section{Physics-based routes to binding free energies}

One established route to binding affinity is to estimate free energies directly from molecular simulation. Umbrella sampling is a classical example: it introduces biasing potentials along a chosen coordinate and then reconstructs an unbiased free-energy profile from the reweighted samples \citep{Torrie1977Umbrella}. Alchemical free-energy methods define a thermodynamic path between states and integrate the free-energy change along that path; these methods have matured substantially, especially in the protein--ligand setting \citep{Cournia2017RBFE}. Their attraction is obvious, since they target the quantity of interest directly, but they are also computationally expensive and sensitive to protocol design.

At lower cost, endpoint methods such as MM/PBSA and MM/GBSA combine molecular-mechanics energies with continuum-solvent approximations \citep{Genheden2015MMGBSA}. These methods are widely used and often provide useful comparison points, but their performance depends strongly on solvent treatment, entropy approximations, and state averaging \citep{Genheden2015MMGBSA}. For the present thesis, this literature motivates taking molecular dynamics seriously without making full free-energy estimation the centre of the project. Molecular dynamics is used here as a source of descriptors, not as a direct free-energy engine.

\section{Empirical and learned structure-based affinity models}

Because rigorous simulation is expensive and endpoint methods remain approximate, a parallel literature predicts affinity more directly from structure. PRODIGY is a clear example: it regresses binding affinity from interfacial contact counts and related structural descriptors \citep{Vangone2015Contacts}. Other earlier structure-based models similarly relied on carefully engineered descriptors combined with standard regression models \citep{Moal2011AffinityPrediction}. These approaches are attractive because they are fast and interpretable, but they depend strongly on what has been chosen as input.

More recent work has moved toward learned structural representations. The PPB-Affinity paper includes a benchmark deep model on the new dataset \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}, and more recent studies such as PPI-Graphomer have explored pretrained and graph-based representations for direct affinity prediction \citep{Xie2025PPIGraphomer}. DeepRank2 is especially relevant to the present thesis because it provides a practical framework for residue-level and atom-level graph construction with biophysical node and edge features \citep{Crocioni2024DeepRank2}. The active pipeline in this thesis builds directly on that style of representation rather than on a small fixed descriptor set.

\section{Why the protein--ligand literature is ahead}

The protein--ligand literature provides a useful contrast. Datasets such as PDBbind have given that field a longer and more standardized benchmark tradition \citep{Wang2004PDBbind}. This has supported a wide range of learned structure-based models, including voxelized approaches such as Pafnucy \citep{Stepniewska2018Pafnucy} and more recent trajectory-aware models that learn directly from MD-derived protein--ligand data \citep{Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}. The protein--ligand case therefore shows that learned geometric and dynamic representations can be useful when the benchmark ecosystem is strong enough.

Protein--protein affinity prediction has a less settled representation question. A ligand naturally defines a local binding object inside a pocket, whereas a protein--protein complex immediately raises the question of whether the right representation is the whole assembly, the interface only, or some local patch around the interface. This is one reason the present thesis compares full-complex and interface-only residue graphs directly rather than treating either view as standard.

\section{Variational autoencoders in molecular and structural learning}

Variational autoencoders were introduced as continuous latent-variable models trained by combining reconstruction with variational regularization \citep{Kingma2014VAE}. In molecular machine learning, they became attractive not only for generation but also for representation learning: a VAE can compress high-dimensional structured inputs into a smoother latent space while still preserving information needed for reconstruction or downstream prediction. This idea has been influential in molecular design and molecular graph modelling, including continuous latent-space molecular models and graph-based variational autoencoders \citep{GomezBombarelli2018MolecularVAE,Kipf2016VGAE,Simonovsky2018GraphVAE}.

What matters in the present thesis is not molecular generation itself, but the representational role of the VAE. A variational graph model can use reconstruction as an auxiliary task, regularize the latent space, and still support downstream supervised evaluation. This makes it attractive when the structural input is richer than the label budget alone can exploit reliably. At the same time, VAEs are not a default standard in direct protein--protein affinity prediction. Recent affinity models in this area are still more commonly framed as supervised regressors than as latent-variable representation learners \citep{Guo2022PPBAReview,Xie2025PPIGraphomer}. In that sense, the GraphVAE used here is a deliberate modelling choice rather than a settled convention.

\section{Where this thesis fits}

Taken together, the literature suggests a practical gap rather than a settled recipe. Rigorous free-energy methods are principled but expensive. Fast empirical scorers are useful but compressed. Protein--ligand work shows that learned geometric and dynamic representations can succeed when the benchmark ecosystem is strong enough, but the protein--protein case remains less standardized. Variational latent models are established in molecular representation learning, but they are not yet a standard answer to direct protein--protein affinity prediction.

This thesis sits in that gap. It asks whether short explicit-solvent molecular dynamics can be reduced to graph-compatible descriptors that improve held-out protein--protein affinity prediction relative to a strong static residue graph. The comparison between full and interface graph views, the comparison between static and static-plus-dynamic features, and the comparison between a masked GraphVAE and a direct supervised baseline all follow from that question.
