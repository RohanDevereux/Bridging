\chapter{Archived Approaches}
\label{ch:appendix_archive}

This appendix summarises the main alternative approaches explored before the residue-graph study reported in the main chapters. The archived materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE} in the repository and can also be browsed at \url{https://github.com/RohanDevereux/Bridging/tree/main/ARCHIVE}.

\section{Correction learning around fast affinity baselines}

An early idea was to predict a correction
\[
\delta = \Delta G_{\mathrm{exp}} - \Delta G_{\mathrm{base}}
\]
instead of predicting $\Delta G$ directly. Two approaches were explored in this form. One used the contact-based baseline PRODIGY \citep{Vangone2015Contacts}. The other used endpoint free-energy estimates from MM/GBSA \citep{Massova2000MMPBSA}.

\subsection{PRODIGY}

One approach used PRODIGY estimates as a fast structural baseline and then learned a correction to experimental affinity \citep{Vangone2015Contacts}. The corresponding materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE/PRODIGY}. In the saved runs, raw PRODIGY predictions were weak on the archived TCR-heavy subsets, and direct prediction from learned structural representations was stronger than learning a correction around PRODIGY. The archived subgroup summaries also already hinted that the interaction classes behave differently.

The saved subset-specific baseline outputs showed the same pattern. On an archived TCR-only dataset, PRODIGY was essentially uncorrelated with experiment. On a small antibody--antigen archived dataset, the same baseline was better, though still modest. A mixed 100-complex subset was again weak.

\subsection{MM/GBSA}

A second correction-learning approach used MM/GBSA endpoint estimates \citep{Massova2000MMPBSA}. The corresponding materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE/MMGBSA}. Across the partial result files there were 163 rows with status \texttt{ok}. On those comparable rows, the MM/GBSA estimates had Pearson correlation $-0.127$, Spearman correlation $-0.096$, RMSE 2458.69, and MAE 1398.31. The scale of the predictions was far from the experimental target range: the median successful estimate was 466.38 kcal mol$^{-1}$, the 95th percentile was 5590.71 kcal mol$^{-1}$, and the maximum exceeded 13\,000 kcal mol$^{-1}$, whereas the corresponding experimental free energies lay roughly between $-21$ and $-4$ kcal mol$^{-1}$.

\section{The C\texorpdfstring{$\alpha$}{alpha} contact-map approach}

An earlier model family represented complexes as fixed two-dimensional tensors built from C$\alpha$--C$\alpha$ contact and distance channels, followed by a convolutional variational autoencoder and regression model. The corresponding materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE/CA_contact_map}. This image-like representation was appealing because related ideas have worked in protein--ligand learning \citep{Stepniewska2018Pafnucy}, but in the archived runs it was too unstable for the present task. Some variants reduced RMSE relative to raw PRODIGY on small subsets, yet the same runs often had weak or negative test correlations and varied sharply across seeds. The reported values below are test-set outputs from the direct-latent regression path built on that contact-map representation.

All runs in Table~\ref{tab:appendix-contact} share the same basic contact-map representation and the same set-based edge encoder. The labels mainly distinguish training and downstream-regression variants: \texttt{e24} marks the 24-epoch latent-model checkpoint used for feature extraction, \texttt{seed1} and \texttt{seed4} change the random seed, \texttt{semisup} adds an auxiliary affinity loss during latent-model training, \texttt{ols} records an archived unregularised regression variant, and \texttt{allmd} marks a broader MD-frame variant preserved in the archived results. The spread across those runs shows why this branch was left behind.

Compared with the final residue-graph formulation, the contact-map representation threw away residue identity detail, side-chain context, and explicit graph structure. It still depended on a fixed interface crop and a fixed tensor ordering. The residue-graph formulation retained more structural information and connected more naturally to the MD-derived features used in the main chapters, so the work later shifted away from the contact-map path.

\section{PPB-Affinity interface-attention augmentation}

Another line of work kept more of the trajectory itself, with baseline, frame-level, and trajectory-level models together with an ensemble stage. The corresponding materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE/PPB_Affinity_augmentation}. It reused a vendored PPB-Affinity-style baseline stack, including a geometry-aware interface-attention model, and paired that baseline with MD-frame augmentation and sequence modelling. Here, augmentation meant treating saved MD frames from one complex as additional supervised inputs that all inherited the same affinity label. The explored routes covered a single-structure baseline, frame-level models trained on the augmented examples, and trajectory-level models that passed ordered frame embeddings through a bidirectional gated recurrent unit (BiGRU) before final regression \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity,Schuster1997BRNN,Cho2014GRU}. An ensemble stage was intended to combine predictions from the baseline, frame-level, and trajectory-level routes. Recent protein--ligand work had started to learn from MD frame sequences and to retain temporal order instead of reducing trajectories to simple averages \citep{Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}, which made the same question worth asking for protein--protein affinity prediction.

Work later shifted toward the residue-graph comparison used in the main chapters. The change reflected project focus, not a judgement that the augmentation idea lacked promise. The archived materials remain relevant future work, especially for asking whether MD-frame augmentation or spatiotemporal models can improve graph-based protein--protein affinity prediction.
