\chapter{Archived Approaches and Project Evolution}
\label{ch:appendix_archive}

This appendix gathers the main alternative approaches explored during the project. These archived branches show other directions considered before the final thesis focused on residue graphs with short-MD summary features. The materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE} in the repository and can also be browsed at \url{https://github.com/RohanDevereux/Bridging/tree/main/ARCHIVE}.

\section{Correction learning around fast affinity baselines}

An early project idea was to predict a correction
\[
\delta = \Delta G_{\mathrm{exp}} - \Delta G_{\mathrm{base}}
\]
instead of predicting $\Delta G$ directly. Two approaches were explored in this form: a contact-based baseline using PRODIGY \citep{Vangone2015Contacts} and an endpoint free-energy baseline using MM/GBSA \citep{Massova2000MMPBSA}.

\subsection{PRODIGY}

One approach used PRODIGY estimates as a fast structural baseline and then learned a correction to experimental affinity \citep{Vangone2015Contacts}. The corresponding materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE/PRODIGY}. In the saved runs, raw PRODIGY predictions were weak on the archived TCR-heavy subsets, and direct prediction from learned structural representations was stronger than learning a correction around PRODIGY. The archived subgroup summaries also already hinted that the interaction classes behave differently.

The saved subset-specific baseline outputs showed the same pattern. On an archived TCR-only dataset, PRODIGY was essentially uncorrelated with experiment. On a small antibody--antigen archived dataset, the same baseline was better, though still modest. A mixed 100-complex subset was again weak.

\subsection{MM/GBSA}

A second correction-learning approach used MM/GBSA endpoint estimates \citep{Massova2000MMPBSA}. The corresponding materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE/MMGBSA}. Across the partial result files there were 163 rows with status \texttt{ok}. On those comparable rows, the MM/GBSA estimates had Pearson correlation $-0.127$, Spearman correlation $-0.096$, RMSE 2458.69, and MAE 1398.31. The scale of the predictions was far from the experimental target range: the median successful estimate was 466.38 kcal mol$^{-1}$, the 95th percentile was 5590.71 kcal mol$^{-1}$, and the maximum exceeded 13\,000 kcal mol$^{-1}$, whereas the corresponding experimental free energies lay roughly between $-21$ and $-4$ kcal mol$^{-1}$.

\section{The C\texorpdfstring{$\alpha$}{alpha} contact-map approach}

An earlier model family represented complexes as fixed two-dimensional tensors built from C$\alpha$--C$\alpha$ contact and distance channels, followed by a convolutional variational autoencoder and regression model. The corresponding materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE/CA_contact_map}. This image-like representation was appealing because related ideas have worked in protein--ligand learning \citep{Stepniewska2018Pafnucy}, but in the archived runs it was too unstable for the present task. Some variants reduced RMSE relative to raw PRODIGY on small subsets, yet the same runs often had weak or negative test correlations and varied sharply across seeds. The reported values below are test-set outputs from the direct-latent regression path built on that contact-map representation.

Table~\ref{tab:appendix-contact} reports representative direct-latent regressors from that family. The run names differ by training schedule, regression variant, or random seed, but they share the same basic contact-map representation. The spread across those runs explains why the final comparison moved on from this path.

Compared with the final residue-graph formulation, the contact-map representation threw away residue identity detail, side-chain context, and explicit graph structure while still depending on a fixed interface crop and a fixed tensor ordering. The residue-graph formulation retained more structural information and connected more naturally to the MD-derived features used in the main text, so the project later shifted away from the contact-map path.

\section{PPB-Affinity interface-attention augmentation}

Another line of work kept more of the trajectory itself, with baseline, frame-level, and trajectory-level models together with an ensemble stage. The corresponding materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE/PPB_Affinity_augmentation}. It reused a vendored PPB-Affinity-style baseline stack as its starting point, including the same kind of geometry-aware attention model over an interface patch, and then changed the data path around it. Here, augmentation meant treating saved MD frames from one complex as additional supervised inputs that all inherited the same affinity label. The branch then explored three linked routes on top of that PPB-style interface model: a single-structure baseline, frame-level models trained on those augmented examples, and trajectory-level models that passed ordered frame embeddings through a bidirectional gated recurrent unit (BiGRU) before final regression \citep{Schuster1997BRNN,Cho2014GRU}. Recent protein--ligand work had started to learn from MD frame sequences rather than from simple averages \citep{Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}, so the branch tested whether the same idea could transfer to protein--protein affinity prediction.

The branch remained exploratory after the final comparison shifted toward the residue-graph study used in the main text. The shift says little about the augmentation idea itself. It remains relevant as a follow-on direction because it preserves temporal order that the final summary-feature approach discards.
