\chapter{Archived Approaches}
\label{ch:appendix_archive}

This appendix summarises the main alternative approaches explored before the residue-graph study reported in the main chapters. The aim is to show what each branch was trying to test, what the provisional results suggested, and why project focus later shifted toward the final residue-graph comparison. The archived materials remain available under \path{ARCHIVE} in the repository.

\section{Correction learning around fast affinity baselines}

An early idea was to predict a correction
\[
\delta = \Delta G_{\mathrm{exp}} - \Delta G_{\mathrm{base}}
\]
instead of predicting $\Delta G$ directly. That strategy only makes sense if the baseline already captures a meaningful part of the affinity signal. Two approaches were explored in this form. One used the contact-based baseline PRODIGY \citep{Vangone2015Contacts}. The other used endpoint free-energy estimates from MM/GBSA \citep{Massova2000MMPBSA}. In practice, neither baseline met that requirement, so later work shifted toward predicting absolute binding affinity directly.

\subsection{PRODIGY}

One approach used PRODIGY estimates as a fast structural baseline and then learned a correction to experimental affinity \citep{Vangone2015Contacts}. The idea was plausible because PRODIGY is quick, interpretable, and already tailored to protein--protein complexes, so a learned model might only need to capture the systematic error left over after a reasonable first estimate. On the archived TCR-heavy subsets, however, that starting point was weak, and direct prediction from learned structural representations was stronger than learning a correction around PRODIGY. That pattern is consistent with later evidence that PRODIGY can look much stronger on easier benchmark subsets than on separate hold-out evaluation sets \citep{RomeroMolina2022PPIAffinity}. The likely reason is that a contact-based baseline is most useful when it already tracks the broad structure of the target. On the harder subsets here, especially the TCR-rich ones, the baseline was already too weak for the residual to become a simpler learning problem.

The subset-specific baseline results showed the same pattern. On an archived TCR-only dataset, PRODIGY was essentially uncorrelated with experiment. On a small antibody--antigen dataset, the same baseline was better, though still modest. A mixed 100-complex subset was again weak.

\subsection{MM/GBSA}

MM/GBSA endpoint estimates provided a second correction-learning baseline \citep{Massova2000MMPBSA}. This idea was also reasonable at first sight, because a more explicitly physics-based baseline might have captured energetic structure that a contact heuristic could not, leaving a smaller and more learnable residual. The archived MM/GBSA estimates were too poorly calibrated for that role. On 163 comparable estimates, Pearson correlation was $-0.127$, Spearman correlation was $-0.096$, RMSE was 2458.69, and MAE was 1398.31. More importantly, the predicted values lay far outside the experimental target range: the median estimate was 466.38 kcal mol$^{-1}$, the 95th percentile was 5590.71 kcal mol$^{-1}$, and the maximum exceeded 13\,000 kcal mol$^{-1}$, whereas the corresponding experimental free energies lay roughly between $-21$ and $-4$ kcal mol$^{-1}$. That scale failure suggests a mismatch between the method and the present benchmark, not just ordinary noise. The interpretation is more straightforward for this branch. For protein--protein binding, endpoint MM/PBSA or MM/GBSA methods are already challenging because the binding free energy is obtained as a small residual of large energetic contributions and omitted entropic terms can change the estimate substantially \citep{Massova2000MMPBSA,Siebenmorgen2020PPIReview}. In this branch the calculation was also kept deliberately lightweight, using a GB endpoint setup on short subsampled trajectories without an explicit entropy correction, so the branch never produced a baseline that was quantitatively useful for correction learning.

Taken together, the two baseline branches answered the same practical question. Correction learning was not the right starting point here, because neither baseline captured affinity well enough to make the residual easier to learn than absolute binding affinity itself. PRODIGY was weak on the harder subsets, and MM/GBSA was badly calibrated on the present benchmark. Later work therefore focused on predicting absolute binding affinity directly.

\section{The C\texorpdfstring{$\alpha$}{alpha} contact-map approach}

An earlier model family represented complexes as fixed two-dimensional tensors built from C$\alpha$--C$\alpha$ contact and distance channels, followed by a convolutional variational autoencoder and regression model. This image-like representation was appealing because related ideas have worked in protein--ligand learning \citep{Stepniewska2018Pafnucy}. The archived results, however, were too unstable for the present task. Some variants reduced RMSE relative to raw PRODIGY on small subsets, yet the same runs often had weak or negative test correlations and varied sharply across seeds. Table~\ref{tab:appendix-contact} summarises representative test-set results from the direct-latent regression path built on that contact-map representation.

All runs in Table~\ref{tab:appendix-contact} share the same basic contact-map representation and the same set-based edge encoder. The variants differ in checkpoint choice, random seed, supervision strategy, downstream regression, and the breadth of MD-frame coverage. That spread is useful because it shows the character of the branch: there were hints of lower error on some small test slices, but the direction was not robust enough to justify taking it forward.

Compared with the final residue-graph formulation, the contact-map representation threw away residue identity detail, side-chain context, and explicit graph structure. It still depended on a fixed interface crop and a fixed tensor ordering. The residue-graph formulation retained more structural information and connected more naturally to the MD-derived features used in the main chapters, so the work later shifted away from the contact-map path.

\section{PPB-Affinity interface-attention augmentation}

Another line of work kept more of the trajectory itself, with baseline, frame-level, and trajectory-level models together with an ensemble stage. It stayed closer to the PPB-Affinity benchmark family by reusing a geometry-aware interface-attention model and then asking two broader questions. First, could saved MD frames act as useful supervised augmentation, with each frame inheriting the parent complex affinity label? Second, could ordered frame embeddings support a trajectory-level predictor that preserved temporal order instead of collapsing the trajectory to a few averages? The explored routes therefore included a single-structure baseline, frame-level models trained on augmented MD examples, and trajectory-level models that passed ordered frame embeddings through a bidirectional gated recurrent unit (BiGRU) before final regression \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity,Schuster1997BRNN,Cho2014GRU}. An ensemble stage was intended to combine the baseline, frame-level, and trajectory-level routes. Recent protein--ligand work had started to learn from MD frame sequences and to retain temporal order instead of reducing trajectories to simple averages \citep{Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}, which made the same question worth asking for protein--protein affinity prediction.

This branch was not run through to the same full comparison as the residue-graph study in the main chapters because project focus shifted toward graphs with dynamic features. The change reflected scope and priorities, not a judgement that the augmentation idea lacked promise. The archived materials remain relevant future work, especially for asking whether MD-frame augmentation or spatiotemporal models can improve graph-based protein--protein affinity prediction.
