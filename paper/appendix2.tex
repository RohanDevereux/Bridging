\chapter{Archived Approaches and Project Evolution}
\label{ch:appendix_archive}

This appendix records the main alternative approaches that were explored seriously during the project but did not become part of the final study. They are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE} in the repository and can also be browsed on GitHub at \url{https://github.com/RohanDevereux/Bridging/tree/main/ARCHIVE}.

\section{Correction learning around fast affinity baselines}

An early project idea was to predict a correction
\[
\delta = \Delta G_{\mathrm{exp}} - \Delta G_{\mathrm{base}}
\]
rather than predict $\Delta G$ directly. Two approaches were explored in this form: a contact-based baseline using PRODIGY \citep{Vangone2015Contacts} and an endpoint free-energy baseline using MM/GBSA \citep{Genheden2015MMGBSA}.

\subsection{PRODIGY}

One approach used PRODIGY estimates as a fast structural baseline and then learned a correction to experimental affinity \citep{Vangone2015Contacts}. The corresponding materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE/PRODIGY}.

On the preserved TCR subset runs, raw PRODIGY predictions were poor. For the common 13-complex test subset in the preserved results, PRODIGY gave test RMSE 10.50 and test Pearson correlation $-0.268$. Some learned latent regressors reduced RMSE relative to PRODIGY on the overlapping test cases, but correction-to-PRODIGY was not competitive with direct latent regression.

The saved subset-specific baseline results also showed that the interaction classes behaved differently. On a TCR-only archived dataset, PRODIGY was essentially uncorrelated with experiment. On a small antibody--antigen archived dataset, the same baseline was better, though still modest. A mixed 100-complex subset was again poor.

\subsection{MM/GBSA}

A second correction-learning approach used MM/GBSA endpoint estimates \citep{Massova2000MMPBSA,Genheden2015MMGBSA}. The corresponding materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE/MMGBSA}.

Across the shard files there were 163 rows with status \texttt{ok}. On the 163 rows that could be compared against the experimental PPB-Affinity target, the MM/GBSA estimates had Pearson correlation $-0.127$, Spearman correlation $-0.096$, RMSE 2458.69, and MAE 1398.31. The numerical scale was pathological: the median successful estimate was 466.38 kcal mol$^{-1}$, the 95th percentile was 5590.71 kcal mol$^{-1}$, and the maximum exceeded 13\,000 kcal mol$^{-1}$, whereas the corresponding experimental free energies lay roughly between $-21$ and $-4$ kcal mol$^{-1}$.

\section{The C$\alpha$ contact-map approach}

An earlier model family represented complexes as fixed two-dimensional tensors built from C$\alpha$--C$\alpha$ contact and distance channels, followed by a convolutional variational autoencoder and regression model. The corresponding materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE/CA_contact_map}.

A contact map gives a fixed-size, image-like representation that is much easier to batch and process than a variable-size protein graph. That design choice has close relatives in protein--ligand learning, where voxelized or tensorized structural inputs have been used successfully \citep{Stepniewska2018Pafnucy}.

The preserved results show lower RMSE than raw PRODIGY on some runs, but not a stable predictor. For example, the \texttt{setedge\_e24} run gave test RMSE 2.80 against PRODIGY's 10.50 on the same subset, and the \texttt{setedge\_e24\_ols} variant gave test RMSE 2.76. The same runs often had negative test correlations, and a five-seed sweep of the \texttt{setedge\_e24} regressor showed substantial instability: test RMSE ranged from 2.80 to 6.62 and test Pearson correlation ranged from $-0.500$ to 0.462. Semi-supervised training and correction-to-PRODIGY were not improvements in the preserved results.

Once the residue-graph study was in place, the contact-map representation looked too compressed: it discarded residue identity detail, side-chain context, and explicit graph structure, while still depending on a fixed interface selection and a fixed tensor ordering. The preserved results also showed a method that could sometimes reduce error but did not do so in a stable way. The residue-graph formulation retained more structural information and connected more naturally to the MD-derived features studied in the main text.

\section{The PPB-inspired augmentation and spatiotemporal approach}

Another line of work aimed to use MD more directly, with baseline, frame-level, and trajectory-level models together with an ensemble stage. The corresponding materials are preserved under \path{ARCHIVE/PPB_Affinity_augmentation}.

This path tried to use MD more directly by learning from frames and trajectories rather than reducing the trajectory to a small set of graph-level summaries. PPB-Affinity was introduced as a machine-learning resource for protein drug discovery \citep{Liu2024PPBAffinity}, and recent protein--ligand work has shown that spatio-temporal learning on MD-derived data can be effective in some settings \citep{Libouban2025Spatiotemporal}. The motivation was to test whether that style of learning could transfer to protein--protein affinity prediction, where the interfaces are larger, the labelled benchmarks are less standardised, and the structural heterogeneity is broader \citep{Wang2004PDBbind,Kastritis2011Benchmark,Liu2024PPBAffinity}.

This approach does not have a preserved final benchmark summary comparable with the residue-graph study. The intended training setup was computationally heavy, so it was not run through to a comparable evaluation. It remains a plausible future direction.
